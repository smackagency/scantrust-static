Why secure QR codes are the future of anti-counterfeiting

Peter Kostur, Sales & Partnerships Director EMEA, walks you through some of the pros and cons of different types of anti-counterfeiting technology; covert vs. overt and physical vs. digital, emphasizing secure QR codes.

How do you protect your customers, brand, and products from counterfeiting? What is important for such solutions that provide that protection? Four words: security, usability, scalability, and utility. There are many physical and digital anti-counterfeiting technologies available, but which is the right one for your company? To understand some of the pros and cons of different types of anti-counterfeiting technology this article discusses:

  1. How secure QR codes work as an anti-counterfeiting technology
  2. Covert vs. overt technologies
  3. Physical vs. digital technologies

How secure QR codes work as an anti-counterfeiting technology

Secure QR codes are unlike standard QR codes because they include a copy detection feature that is the basis for their usefulness as anti-counterfeiting tools. Standard QR codes are designed to be very easy to print from a source digital file. This, in turn, makes them easy to replicate as a data carrier that is very useful for marketing, logistics, and any application where a user needs quick access to online information. Secure QR codes take advantage of those properties and add a copy detection layer to get the best of both worlds. Here are a few characteristics of secure QR codes:

  • Instant authentication result using a smartphone
  • Familiarity and ease of use for consumers: QR code usage is universal.
  • No need for special phone apps
  • Can be used anywhere existing marketing and supply chain tools are using QR codes or similar tools

Covert vs. Overt Technologies

Overt anti-counterfeiting is typified by a feature than anyone can observe without special tools, devices, or training. An overt anti-counterfeiting tool is difficult to reproduce and may include physical security features such as a tamper-proof seal. Some examples of overt anti-counterfeiting are holographic labels, color-shifting inks, and micro texts.

Covert anti-counterfeiting solutions contrast with overt anti-counterfeiting in that they are not immediately discernible or observable. They are only visible with specialized tools or techniques. Covert anti-counterfeiting tools are hidden to avoid obvious identification: this is part of the strategy. Some examples of covert anti-counterfeiting are special inks, UV ink that only shines under ultraviolet light, magnetic inks, DNA taggants, and patterns of dots that are distributed randomly across a graphical design. Those are known as sparse patterns. You will also see some copy detection patterns included in the covert anti-counterfeiting category.
